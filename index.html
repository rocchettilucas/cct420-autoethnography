01 — Introduction

The job search is not a neutral process.

The contemporary search for employment in technology is deeply mediated — restructured by platforms, algorithms, and global digital systems that reshape how we present ourselves, measure our competence, and experience waiting, rejection, and hope.

This project is an autoethnographic account of searching for work in tech. Through three scenes — rewriting resumes for applicant tracking systems, practicing algorithmic problems on LeetCode, and navigating the ambient emotional terrain of LinkedIn — it examines how the ordinary routines of job seeking reveal the lived textures of information technology and globalization.

Arjun Appadurai
Technoscape

The global configuration of technology — a fluid, irregular landscape through which mechanical and informational technology moves at high speed across boundaries. The job search is lived within this landscape: platforms built in one country structure applications submitted from another, evaluated by systems designed elsewhere entirely.

Alessandro Delfanti
Algorithmic Management

Control over workers extends through metrics, rankings, and machine-readable evaluation — a logic that begins not at employment, but at the application itself. Before you are hired, you are already being managed: parsed, scored, filtered, ranked.

What follows is not a summary of an essay. It is an attempt to make the argument felt — to render the mediated job search as an experience you move through, not merely a claim you read.

02 — Scene One

Rewriting the Self for the Machine

Every application begins with translation. Not between languages, but between selves — from the person you are to the candidate the system can read. Applicant tracking systems parse resumes for keyword density, formatting compliance, and algorithmic compatibility. The question is no longer what have you done but can the machine find it.

03 — Scene Two

Performing Competence Under Constraint

Technical skill is not demonstrated through projects, portfolios, or conversation. It is performed — under timed conditions, against invisible global benchmarks, in an interface designed to measure speed, correctness, and efficiency. The platform does not care what you know. It measures what you can produce in the time allotted.

42. Optimal Subsequence Alignment

Given two strings text1 and text2, return the length of their longest common subsequence. If there is no common subsequence, return 0.

A subsequence of a string is a sequence that can be derived from the given string by deleting some or no characters without changing the relative order of remaining characters.

Example:
Input: text1 = "intention", text2 = "execution"
Output: 5
Constraints:
1 ≤ text1.length, text2.length ≤ 1000

var longestCommonSubseq = function(text1, text2) {
    const m = text1.length;
    const n = text2.length;
    const dp = Array(m + 1).fill(null)
        .map(() => Array(n + 1).fill(0));

    for (let i = 1; i <= m; i++) {
        for (let j = 1; j <= n; j++) {
            if (text1[i-1] === text2[j-1]) {
                dp[i][j] = dp[i-1][j-1] + 1;
            } else {
                dp[i][j] = Math.max(dp[i-1][j], dp[i][j-1]);
            }
        }
    }
    return dp[m][n];
};

The solution is accepted. The performance is ranked. Somewhere across the world, another applicant solves the same problem faster. The platform does not know what you understood — only what you produced, and how quickly. Skill becomes legible only as speed and correctness. Everything else is noise the system cannot parse.

04 — Scene Three

The Ambient Search

The job search does not have edges. It does not begin when you open the laptop or end when you close it. LinkedIn ensures that the search is ambient — always running, always producing signals. A profile view at midnight. A rejection at breakfast. A former classmate's celebration in the middle of a lecture. The platform sustains a low-frequency hum of availability, comparison, and waiting.

05 — The Infrastructure

Platforms as Global Architecture

The three scenes are not isolated experiences. They are connected by the same underlying infrastructure — a network of platforms that operate across borders, standardize labour, and produce globally comparable metrics of human value. The job search is where globalization becomes personal: felt not as an abstraction of trade flows, but as a daily encounter with systems that were designed elsewhere, for everyone, and therefore for no one in particular.

technoscape platform labour algorithmic visibility standardization global competition metric legibility

Appadurai wrote that globalization produces disjunctive flows — overlapping but uneven movements of technology, finance, people, media, and ideas. The technoscape is not smooth. The platforms that mediate the job search are products of Silicon Valley venture capital, but they shape applications filed from Toronto, Bangalore, Lagos, and Berlin. Delfanti's algorithmic management does not stop at the warehouse floor; it extends backward into hiring, where applicants are already measured, ranked, and filtered before they ever begin to work.

This is what globalization feels like at the level of the everyday: not a headline about trade policy, but a 37% match score at 2 a.m., a LeetCode percentile compared against ten thousand strangers, and a LinkedIn notification that someone you graduated with just started at a company that rejected you.

06 — Conclusion

After the Algorithm

The mediated job search is not merely a process of finding work. It is a reorganization of selfhood — a rewriting of identity into formats that machines can read, platforms can circulate, and markets can evaluate. The applicant tracking system does not simply screen resumes; it teaches applicants to write themselves differently. LeetCode does not simply test knowledge; it trains a particular kind of performance under artificial constraints. LinkedIn does not simply connect professionals; it produces a state of ambient availability, where the self is always partially on display, always partially searching.

These platforms are not neutral infrastructure. They are the architecture through which global labour markets exert pressure on individual lives — shaping daily routines, compressing identity, and distributing emotional weight across ordinary digital interactions. To search for a job in technology today is to be processed by the same systems that one hopes to build. The subject becomes the object of the algorithm before employment even begins.

You are not just looking for work.
The system is looking at you.
